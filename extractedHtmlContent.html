Binyamin Netanyahu, Israel’s prime minister, said that his government was “making progress” on a deal with Hamas for the release of hostages. He has convened meetings this evening with the war cabinet, security cabinet and government. Earlier Ismail Haniyeh, Hamas’s political chief, said the militant group is “close to reaching a truce agreement” with Israel. The terms reportedly include Israel agreeing to a five-day pause in its offensive in the Gaza Strip in exchange for Hamas returning some of the hostages. Qatar, which is brokering the deal along with America, said that talks over the deal were at a “critical and final stage”.

Almost all of OpenAI’s employees signed an open letter urging the company’s board to resign, after it sacked the company’s boss, Sam Altman, on Friday. Most of OpenAI’s 770 employees threatened to jump ship to Mr Altman’s newly established team at Microsoft (which is also OpenAI’s biggest investor). Emmett Shear, former head of Twitch, a video-streaming service, has been appointed OpenAI’s interim boss.

Xi Jinping, China’s president, called for an international peace conference to resolve the war between Israel and Hamas during an online meeting of the BRICS, an economic bloc. Leaders from Brazil, Russia, India, China and South Africa were joined by those from the group’s soon-to-be new members, including Iran and Saudi Arabia. Cyril Ramaphosa, the president of South Africa, accused Israel of actions “tantamount to genocide” in Gaza.

North Korea fired what is suspected to be a spy satellite, in its third launch attempt this year (both previous attempts failed). If it stays up, the satellite would give the country the means to monitor the movements of American and South Korean troops. Japan, which had condemned the launch after being warned in advance, told citizens on Okinawa, a southern Japanese island, to shelter.

Germany suspended new spending commitments, as the government grapples with a budgetary crisis caused by a controversial court ruling last week. The judgement by the country’s highest court said that unspent government funds, originally earmarked for covid-19, could not be used for other purposes. Germany’s strict borrowing rules, the “debt brake”, leave the government with few other ways to raise funds.

Britain’s Supreme Court ruled that riders for Deliveroo, a food-delivery company, cannot be recognised as employees, in a landmark ruling for the gig economy. The court cited the riders’ ability to choose their own hours and delegate their orders to another person without Deliveroo’s involvement. In 2021 the court ruled that drivers for Uber, a ride-hailing company, were employees.

X (formerly Twitter) sued Media Matters, a watchdog group that last week published a report claiming that prominent brands had seen their advertisements placed next to incendiary and antisemitic tweets. The report triggered an advertiser exodus. X says the ad placements were “manufactured, inorganic and extraordinarily rare”; Media Matters called the lawsuit “frivolous.”
